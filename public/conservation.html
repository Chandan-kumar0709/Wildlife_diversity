Conservation Efforts

Preserving biodiversity through strategic actions and awareness

Why is Wildlife Conservation Necessary?

  • Prevents extinction of endangered species.
  • Maintains ecological balance.
  • Supports biodiversity essential for food, medicine & climate regulation.
  • Boosts eco-tourism and livelihood for rural populations.

Key Government Policies & Acts

  • Wildlife Protection Act (1972): Foundation of legal conservation in India.
  • Forest Conservation Act (1980): Prevents deforestation by regulating diversion.
  • Environment Protection Act (1986): Umbrella law for pollution control and wildlife.
  • CAMPA Fund: Used for compensatory afforestation and biodiversity efforts.

State-wise Conservation Highlights

  • Madhya Pradesh: Known as the Tiger State. Has 11 national parks including Kanha & Bandhavgarh.
  • Assam: Kaziranga National Park is a success story for one-horned rhino conservation.
  • Karnataka: Houses Nagarhole & Bandipur, key habitats for elephants and tigers.
  • Uttarakhand: Home to Jim Corbett National Park, Indiaâ€™s first national park.
  • Chhattisgarh: Focus on tribal participation and dense forest preservation.

Success Stories in India

  • Gir National Park: Home to the last Asiatic lions, whose population has steadily grown.
  • Snow Leopard Project: Started in 2009, covering habitats in J&K, HP, Uttarakhand, Arunachal Pradesh.
  • Amur Falcon Conservation (Nagaland): Migratory bird conservation with community participation.

Future Conservation Strategies

  • Increasing use of technology like drones, AI surveillance & e-patrolling.
  • Greater public-private partnerships for habitat restoration.
  • Strengthening local communities as custodians of forests.
  • Expanding nature education in schools and rural outreach.
